Here's a test I use on any data model: try to say it out loud as a sentence.

> A **user** writes many **posts**. A **post** belongs to one user and has many
> **comments**.

If that narration is smooth, the schema is probably sound. If you find yourself
saying "well, sort of, except when..." — that hesitation is a design smell, and
it almost always shows up later as a confusing feature.

## Nouns are tables, verbs are relationships

Most of modeling is just naming things honestly:

- The **nouns** in your sentence become tables.
- The **verbs** become foreign keys and join tables.
- The **adjectives** become columns.

When a table is hard to name, it usually means it's doing two jobs at once.
Split it until each table is a noun you'd actually use in conversation.

## The product inherits the schema

Users never see your database, but they feel it. A muddy data model leaks out
as muddy navigation, ambiguous states, and features that *almost* work. Clean up
the sentence, and the product gets easier to explain — to your users and to
yourself.
