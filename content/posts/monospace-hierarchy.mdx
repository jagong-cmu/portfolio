A monospace font gives every character the same width. The `i` takes as much
room as the `m`. It sounds like a constraint, and it is — but constraints are
where you learn the most.

When I started using mono for labels and data on this site, I lost my usual
tools for emphasis. You can't lean on dramatic width changes. So I had to find
emphasis somewhere else.

## What's left when size is off the table

- **Space.** Letter-spacing and generous margins do most of the work. Tracking
  out a short uppercase label reads as "this is a heading" without changing the
  size at all.
- **Weight.** A single step from regular to medium is enough to separate a label
  from its value.
- **Case.** Uppercase mono reads as a *system* voice — timestamps, tags, status.
  Sentence case reads as a *human* voice.
- **Rhythm.** Because every glyph aligns to a grid, columns of mono text line up
  on their own. Alignment becomes structure for free.

## The takeaway

Hierarchy isn't about making the important thing bigger. It's about making the
relationships clear. Monospace just takes away the lazy option and forces you to
mean it.
